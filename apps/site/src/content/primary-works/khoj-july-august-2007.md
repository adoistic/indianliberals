- 10 - V 07 Mukeshbhai s complex, multilayered argument is a tough challenge. At first I thought that his argumentwould centre on the theme of marketfailure theory, since he refers to Keynes s theory of market, settling for a level of equilibrium in which unemployment is high.But his focus is on different kind ofmarket failure as it were. He isintrigued and worried about thechoices and preferences many peoplemake. They are wasteful and do notimprove material well-being ofindividuals. The choices are in conflictwith the postulate of rational choicesof economic agents. He supports hiscontention by citing several empiricalobservations in our society and othersocieties. His question is: can market(order) be relied upon to produceefficient outcomes? He then raises thequestion of ethics of marketfunctioning, which he thinks isdominated by empiricism andbehaviourism and not by rationalthinking. Is there a scope of inclusionof rationalist thinking, he asks, to makemarkets more functional?True, man is endowed with reason and rational faculty . But is he always rational? Can any one be rational in the sense that he/she comprehends fullyand can act in that light? Is man aneconomic man who always makesrational economic choices ?Adam Smith and his Scottish friendsnever made such assumptions aboutman. Indeed, in their view, man was by nature lazy , indolent, improvident and wasteful. It is only by the force of circumstances that he begins tobehave economically (rationally), i.e. toadjust carefully his means to his ends.Market order is that circumstance.Market order assumes that man isfree to set his aims, choices etc. Theorder provides a set of institutions inwhich man is induced by his aims andchoices to act in such a way thatcontributes as much as possible to theneeds of others in society. Institutions include private property , which an individual possesses in stable form, which he can use to achieve hisaim, which he can freely dispose off,provided in doing so he doesnt infringeon similar rights of others. And that there is a system of law which enforces a performance of promisesmade.From this, there emerges anorganizational norm. When alternativesare possible, choices are allowed,social (economic) efficiency will begained by channeling mans actions towards mutually compatible goals. Market order is an abstract entity ofrules, norms and mechanisms, whichdoes not and can not guaranteesuccessful and desired outcomes.Market is a process, which, if left free,will gradually approximate to the goalswe desire.Moreover, there is an economic man in each of us along with many other men. His behaviour is an outcome ofso many men interacting within himand between many similar individuals. Even if we dont approve of thechoices of some of us, and to be surethere are many which rattle us, wemust not look for remedies that willundermine the very freedom of choice.This is the hallmark of liberty, an ultimate end and value we must protect at all costs.There is an unalterable andintellectually indisputable fact of theconstitutional limitations of mans knowledge. He can not know more than tiny part of the whole of thesociety. He can know only what af fects him immediately . The real question is not therefore whether man real or apparent; but whether we canallow him to be guided in his actionsby the immediate consequences,which he can know and care for. Or should he be made to do what seems appropriate to somebody else, whoclaims to possess fuller comprehensionof the significance of his actions tosociety as a whole.We must beware of such rational claims, which are prima-facie unfeasible because of our constitutionalignorance and which, if acted upon,can under-mine market order , free society and freedom of men. -Anil Patel -
