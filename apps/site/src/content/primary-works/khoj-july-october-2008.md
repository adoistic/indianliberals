I,however, find several objections to the claims made by rationalists. Rationalists argue that universe is an orderly and organisedplace working on certain principles and human intellect issufficient to discover those principles. Hence, they claim,there is no need for any belief in supernatural. It followsnaturally that the only eligible basis for human action shouldbe rational scientific thinking and nothing else. The debatein this regard, though old, has recently gatheredmomentum in view of the overture of the US Governmenttowards Creationism. First objection is that rationalists are yet to define their domain. Even if we believe that everyone should berational, where and in what context should we be rational?Should our choice of music, choice of spouse, or the waywe should behave with our children or elders, be based on rationality? If so, on what basis such rationality wouldrest? I dont think there are any established rules for that.Sure one of the pillars of rationalism is pluralism, but suchpluralism, by definition, does not include beliefs that arenot scientifically derived. So, how does one find basis forones action in areas where science is yet to establish truths? Second, to the extent that we have likes and dislikes, so faras we love and hate, and that we have biases andpreferences, all of us cease to be rational.
